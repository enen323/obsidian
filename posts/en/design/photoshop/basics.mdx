## You Don't Need to Learn Every Feature Before You Start

About 80% of first-time Photoshop users close the app within five minutes—not because the software is too hard, but because the interface is overwhelming. Twenty-three panel docks, 60+ tools, thousands of options stacked across the screen, and all you wanted was to brighten a photo. Most beginner guides spend a page explaining what each panel does. You memorize the names, open the software, and still have no idea where to click first.

This tutorial skips the "what every button does" lecture. You will complete one real task—opening a photo and exporting a finished result—and along the way learn which tool solves which problem, how layers actually work, and how to adjust color without ruining the original. The whole thing takes under 30 minutes.

## Preparation

**Software Requirements**
- Adobe Photoshop 2024 (25.0+) or CC 2019 or newer
- No third-party plugins required
- Adobe ID needed (7-day free trial available)

**Color Space Setup**
- Go to `Edit > Color Settings`, set Working Space to `sRGB IEC61966-2.1`
- Why sRGB: it is the universal standard for web and social media. What you see on screen is what users see on their devices, with no color mismatch

**File Preparation**
- Download one JPEG photo (1920x1080 resolution or higher recommended)
- Camera original or phone original works best—unedited photos give the cleanest learning experience

**Essential Controls**
- Left mouse button: select tools, click buttons
- Right mouse button: open context menu
- `Ctrl+Z`: undo one step (Photoshop defaults to a single undo; use `Ctrl+Alt+Z` for multiple undos)
- `Space` + drag: pan the canvas
- `Ctrl + scroll wheel`: zoom in / out

## Core Workflow

### Step 1: Create a Document and Import Your Photo

Open Photoshop and press `Ctrl+N` to create a new document:

| Setting | Value | Why |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Width | 1920 px | Web standard width |
| Height | 1080 px | 16:9 aspect ratio |
| Resolution | 72 ppi | Screen display standard |
| Color Mode | RGB Color 8-bit | Web-compatible |
| Background Contents | White | Easy to see changes |

Click "Create", then go to `File > Place Embedded`, select your photo, and press Enter.

**Why 72 ppi instead of 300 ppi?** 300 ppi is for print; 72 ppi is for screens. On a monitor both look identical, but the 72 ppi file is about one-quarter the size. For web-only use, 72 ppi is the correct choice.

### Step 2: Crop the Composition

Press `C` to switch to the Crop tool. In the options bar:
- Set the aspect ratio to `16:9`
- Check "Content-Aware" (fills empty areas created by the crop)

Drag the crop edges to adjust the composition and press Enter. The photo should now fill the canvas edge to edge.

**Why 16:9?** It is the most common aspect ratio for monitors, phone screens, and social media covers. Using a standard ratio ensures your work displays correctly everywhere with no black bars or unexpected cropping.

### Step 3: Extract the Subject with the Object Selection Tool

Press `W` to select the Object Selection tool. Confirm the mode is set to "Rectangle" in the options bar. Draw a rectangle around your main subject—Photoshop's Adobe Sensei AI engine automatically identifies and selects it.

To refine the selection:
- Hold `Shift` and draw around missed areas to add to the selection
- Hold `Alt` and draw around over-selected areas to subtract from the selection

When you are satisfied, press `Ctrl+J` to copy the selection to a new layer. Your Layers panel now shows at least two layers: the original background at the bottom and the extracted subject above it.

**Why Object Selection instead of the Magic Wand?** Object Selection uses AI-based recognition and achieves 90-95% accuracy on people, animals, vehicles, and other well-defined subjects. The Magic Wand relies on color similarity alone and drops to 40-60% accuracy on complex backgrounds such as foliage gaps or hair edges.

### Step 4: Adjust Color with Adjustment Layers

At the bottom of the Layers panel, click the "Create new fill or adjustment layer" button (the half-black, half-white circle). Add these in order:

**Brightness / Contrast**
- Brightness: +15
- Contrast: +10

**Hue / Saturation**
- Saturation: +8

**Why adjustment layers instead of pressing Ctrl+M for Curves?** Adjustment layers are non-destructive—you can double-click the thumbnail to tweak values or delete the adjustment layer entirely, and the original pixels stay untouched. Direct adjustments permanently modify the image with no clean way to revert.

At this point the image should look brighter and more vibrant while skin tones remain natural. If the effect is too strong, double-click the adjustment layer thumbnail and lower the values.

### Step 5: Export for Web

Press `Ctrl+Alt+Shift+S` to open the "Export As" dialog:

| Setting | Value | Why |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Format | JPEG | Best for photos, smooth color transitions |
| Quality | 80% | Visually lossless |
| Convert to sRGB | Checked | Consistent color across devices |
| Width | 1920 px | Standard web size |

Click "Export" and choose a save location.

**Why 80% quality instead of 100%?** The human eye can barely tell the difference between 80% and 100% JPEG quality, but the file is 3 to 5 times smaller. A 1920px photo at 100% quality runs about 1.5-2 MB; at 80% it runs 300-500 KB and loads 3-4 times faster with negligible visual difference.

## Troubleshooting

### Jagged Selection Edges

- **Symptom**: The extracted subject has pixelated "stair-step" edges
- **Root cause**: The selection was not feathered, creating a hard transition
- **Fix**: Press `Shift+F6` to open the Feather Selection dialog and enter 1 px. Values above 2 px will blur the edge noticeably

### Adjustment Layer Has No Effect

- **Symptom**: After adding a Brightness/Contrast adjustment layer, nothing changes
- **Root cause**: The adjustment layer's mask may be filled with black (for example from an accidental brush stroke), hiding the effect entirely
- **Fix**: Click the mask thumbnail on the adjustment layer, then press `Ctrl+Delete` to fill it with white. If there are black marks, paint over them with a white brush

### Exported JPEG Looks Different On Screen

- **Symptom**: The image appears washed out or color-shifted in the browser
- **Root cause**: You edited in Adobe RGB or ProPhoto RGB but did not convert to sRGB on export
- **Fix**: Always check "Convert to sRGB" in the Export As dialog. Better yet, set your workspace to sRGB in `Edit > Color Settings` to prevent the issue entirely

### Ctrl+Z Undoes Too Many Steps

- **Symptom**: Pressing Ctrl+Z skips back multiple steps instead of one
- **Root cause**: Photoshop defaults to a toggle undo (undo/redo on the same key). Users coming from other software often expect continuous undo
- **Fix**: Go to `Edit > Keyboard Shortcuts` and remap "Redo/Undo" to "Step Backward" to match the behavior of other Adobe apps

## Expert Tips

- **Name your layers.** Get in the habit of double-clicking a layer name and typing something meaningful such as "Subject" or "Background". In a 30+ layer PSD file, unnamed layers cost 5-10 minutes of hunting time on every edit session
- **Smart Objects preserve quality.** When you place an image, right-click the layer and choose "Convert to Smart Object." You can scale it up and down repeatedly without losing a single pixel, which is essential for layout exploration
- **Space-drag is 3x faster than scroll bars.** Panning with Space+drag is the most-used shortcut among professional designers. Once it becomes muscle memory, you will never go back to the scroll bars
- **File size reference.** A typical web-use PSD (1920x1080, 5-8 layers) runs 20-50 MB. If your file exceeds 200 MB, check for unoptimized high-resolution images and consider downsampling
